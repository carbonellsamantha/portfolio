Context & Challenge

The Publix app's checkout only accepted credit or debit cards, creating friction for customers and limiting conversion. After leading a payment-preference study, the Product Manager and I advocated for adding Apple Pay. Leadership quickly approved the proposal and requested a three-month launch.

The expectation was to layer Apple Pay into the existing flow. However, as I began working with product, engineering, and analytics, it became clear the issue wasn't just payment—the checkout experience itself was fundamentally broken.

💡

Research

Analytics, usability testing, and competitive research revealed two critical problems that were causing significant customer friction and revenue loss.

Key Findings

➊
High abandonment at the final step 30% of users backtracked from Step 4 → 3, and 10% abandoned entirely.
➋
Slow completion times (5–10 minutes) Customers selecting the earliest pickup time often exceeded that window while reviewing their order, triggering an error and forcing them to restart checkout.

To deliver on leadership's timeline while addressing the broader opportunity, I implemented Apple Pay within the existing system—then used that momentum to reframe the problem and advocate for deeper improvements.

✨

Solution

After delivering the Apple Pay launch, I partnered with the PM to define two follow-up initiatives that addressed both immediate wins and structural issues. Both were approved by leadership.

➊ Express Single-Item Purchase

Enable single-item purchases directly from product detail pages using Apple Pay.

Insight86% of sub orders contained only one item
Business GoalIncrease pay-online adoption
Customer GoalFaster checkout

➋ Checkout Redesign

Move toward a near 1-click checkout by removing unnecessary steps and preselecting reliable defaults (store, pickup window, payment).

Business GoalIncrease conversion, reduce time and abandonment
Customer GoalSimpler, faster checkout

Key Design Decisions

1 of 8
1 of 8

Required Intermediary Step

Apple Pay could not capture required order details (store, time, contact info).

I introduced a lightweight intermediary step before triggering Apple Pay—ensuring all required inputs were captured without breaking flow. This constraint became a design opportunity.

2 of 8

Unified Pattern Across Both Projects

The intermediary step mirrored core checkout actions (store, time, etc.), revealing an opportunity to unify both experiences.

We consolidated into a single reusable pattern that improved consistency, reduced engineering complexity, and enabled parallel development.

3 of 8

Flexible Pickup Windows

60% of users selected the earliest pickup time, but this created a failure case when time expired mid-checkout.

We replaced exact time selection with "As soon as possible (30–45 min after checkout)" which eliminated timeout errors, matched user intent, and maintained optional scheduling.

4 of 8

15-Minute Pickup Range

Customers were frustrated when orders weren't ready at an exact time.

We shifted from "12:15 PM" to "12:15–12:30 PM" which better aligned with real operations and reduced perceived failure.

5 of 8

De-Emphasizing Pay In-Store

Operational data showed waste from unpaid, uncollected orders.

We nested "Pay in store" and positioned Apple Pay as default. Usability testing confirmed discoverability remained intact, so we moved forward confidently.

6 of 8

Balancing Structure vs. Simplicity

Testing showed old flow had clarity (step-by-step) while new flow had speed + preference.

We combined strengths with an accordion-based structure that reduced visual weight while maintaining visibility of key info.

7 of 8

Flexible, Non-Linear Checkout

Testing compared step-locked flow vs. prefilled review-first approach.

The flexible version won across speed, ease, and preference—validating the shift toward 1-click checkout behavior.

8 of 8

Store Confirmation Tradeoff

20% of users changed stores during checkout, so removing confirmation risked misrouted orders.

We kept it temporarily and planned an A/B test to move it earlier in the journey—managing risk while planning for optimization.

Reflections
